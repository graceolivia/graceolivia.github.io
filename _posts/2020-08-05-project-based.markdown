My fiancé and I are both learning to program. He is attending a bachelor's degree in computer science. I am unschooling  at Recurse Center.

We're both learning, both training for jobs as software developers. Today, he laboriously transcribes eigenvectors into Latex. Meanwhile, I am making a quaint little PyGame. I'm building out a portfolio and hoping for the best. He's conquering a thicket of calculus, grad seminars, and extracurriculars.

The bad thing about self-teaching is that you don't always know what's important to focus on. But when you're doing Python projects, some things just keep coming up. Eventually, you learn them because you have to learn them to do the thing you want to do. Example: I don't have much formal training on Object Oriented Programming. But classes keep coming up, and as I start to venture into making a PyGame game, I'm starting to appreciate why they're good. A game character object, that needs to have both data and functions stored, is a perfect use for a class. 

My fiancé is getting a very formal, bottom-up introduction to the concepts of computer science. He will no doubt understand them more thoroughly on a conceptual level than me. I'm taking a project-based approach. It's enjoyable, and keeps me interested, although it involves some dead ends sometimes. I hope that within a few years, we will both be wizards of programming. Or at the very least, able to automate some stuff and make a decent Stardew Valley clone.
